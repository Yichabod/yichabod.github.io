Feeling into this moment, there’s a sad recognition of how spatially distant my community is. For the past year I’ve been living under the kindness of family and friends, lugging my bags and instrument from plane to plane and guest bed to carpeted floor. I’m about to move into my old apartment in Central Square, where I will be in the same room for three months — the longest stretch of stillness I’ve seen.

My family is scattered around the world, and my friends flit from city to city like hungry ghosts. Sometimes it’s wonderful. For my weekend sojourns in Manhattan, there are always friends to talk with — typically between appointments in the nooks of cafes, shielded from the desperate street clamoring. Other times my heart is yarn stretched thin, diaphanous. In the moment right after I call Tuomas, I yearn to be near him, rather than 500 miles away.

Without globalization, I never would have met my current entourage. With globalization, we are drawn to different super-cities by varied faceless institutions. I want my friends all to live in a tree-flanked metropolis by the sea side. I want us to sing songs by a campfire and laugh into the crickets and the croaking of the gloam. I think this can happen, but not right now, and not all the time. I just wish us to be near one another for longer stretches of time.
